How the Darkpool Protocol works

The primary technical goal of the Darkpool Protocol is to enable a decentralized network of nodes to match orders, without knowing anything about the orders. While it might seem like this is impossible, it can be achieved by applying cryptographic techniques that have been thoroughly researched over that last 30 years; modifying them to be suitable for the world of decentralized computation.

Decentralized Order Matching

Order matching is the process through which nodes match orders against each other without being able to observe the details of the order. To achieve this, traders first breakup their order into a set of order fragments

Security Model

The ideal Darkpool Protocol contains a trusted, and incorruptible, third-party T. Traders submit their orders to T, and T guarantees to never reveal the details of these orders.

What is DarkPool?
The purpose of DarkPool Protocol is to build a distributed business application ecosystem of a commercial blockchain, provide blockchain operation products for various industry sectors, and create a high-performance, highly available and easily scalable commercial-grade underlying blockchain platform.
Why does DarkPool exist?
In the traditional technology environment, investors are inevitably exposed when they carry out large transactions, and large amounts of capital being traded will directly affect the entire market. DarkPool open source protocol and blockchain basic services with high performance, high reliability and high scalability can quickly build and meet the application scenarios of large-scale users and maintain the stability of the market under the scenarios of large-scale transactions and high-frequency transactions.
What main points does DarkPool solve?
DarkPool Protocol is a distributed and autonomous organization operating format. It solves the problems of blockchain being directly applied to various complex business application scenarios, the dilemma of mass data storage in large enterprises and institutions, the security risk of user private keys, the cost of enterprise on chain application docking, and the definitions of privacy protection and authority control.
